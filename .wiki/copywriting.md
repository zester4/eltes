# Copywriting

*Practitioner-grade conversion copy frameworks. Read this before writing any landing page, ad, email, or sales asset.*

---

## The Foundation: Eugene Schwartz's 5 Awareness Levels

The single most important framework in copywriting. Match your message to where the reader is.

| Level | What they know | Lead with |
|---|---|---|
| **Unaware** | Don't know they have the problem | A story, a shocking fact, a provocative question |
| **Problem Aware** | Know the problem, not the solution | Name and amplify the pain |
| **Solution Aware** | Know solutions exist, not yours | Your mechanism — why yours is different |
| **Product Aware** | Know your product, not convinced | Proof, risk reversal, urgency |
| **Most Aware** | Ready to buy | Offer, price, CTA |

**Rule:** Most copy fails because it's written for "Most Aware" but served to "Unaware." Cold traffic needs a different lead than warm traffic.

## Core Frameworks

### PAS — Problem, Agitate, Solution
Best for: ads, email subject lines, short copy
1. Name the **Problem** specifically (not generically)
2. **Agitate** — make the reader feel the pain viscerally
3. Present the **Solution** as relief

### AIDA — Attention, Interest, Desire, Action
Best for: long-form sales pages, email sequences
1. **Attention** — hook in the first line or subject line
2. **Interest** — expand on what makes this relevant to them
3. **Desire** — paint the outcome they want; use proof
4. **Action** — single, clear CTA with no friction

### PASTOR (advanced PAS)
Best for: sales pages, VSLs, long-form email
- **P**roblem — name it precisely
- **A**mplify — consequences of not solving it
- **S**tory — proof through narrative
- **T**ransformation — before/after
- **O**ffer — what you're selling
- **R**esponse — the CTA

## The Hook (First Line Rule)

The first line's job is to get them to read the second line. Nothing else.

**Proven hook formulas:**
- **Curiosity gap:** "Most [target] don't know this exists..."
- **Counterintuitive:** "Stop [conventional advice]. Here's what actually works."
- **Social proof:** "X [credible people] switched to this because..."
- **Fear:** "If you're still doing [X], read this before [consequence]"
- **Result:** "How I [specific result] in [specific time] without [common objection]"
- **Direct challenge:** "This email is only for [very specific person]."

**What kills hooks:**
- Starting with "I" (nobody cares about you until they trust you)
- Generic openers ("In today's world...", "Are you tired of...")
- Burying the hook — leading with company/product before the hook

## Voice and Specificity

**The specificity rule:** Replace every vague claim with the most specific version.
- Bad: "Our software saves you time"
- Good: "Teams using this cut their reporting time from 4 hours to 23 minutes"

**The one-reader rule:** Write to a single specific person, not a segment. Use "you" not "you all."

**Conversational register:** Copy that reads like a smart friend explaining something converts better than corporate copy. Read it aloud — if it sounds like a brochure, rewrite it.

**Banned words and phrases:** (remove these on every pass)
- "World-class", "industry-leading", "cutting-edge"
- "Hope this finds you well"
- "As per my previous email"
- "Synergy", "leverage", "ecosystem"
- "I wanted to reach out"
- "At the end of the day"
- Any sentence starting with "We are excited to..."

## The Big Idea

Every great piece of copy is built on a single Big Idea — one counterintuitive, emotionally compelling central claim that the entire piece proves.

Finding your Big Idea:
1. What does your audience believe that's wrong?
2. What does your product prove is possible that they thought wasn't?
3. What's the mechanism — the *why it works* — that's unique?

The Big Idea is the enemy of features. Features support the Big Idea. They are never the Big Idea.

---

## Proof Hierarchy (most to least powerful)

1. **Demonstrated results** — show it working (screenshot, video, live demo)
2. **Specific case studies** — real person, real numbers, real before/after
3. **Third-party validation** — press, experts, certifications
4. **Testimonials** — specific > vague ("I saved 3 hours a week" beats "It's amazing")
5. **Logic** — explanation of why it works mechanically
6. **Your claims** — least powerful; never lead with these

## Objection Handling

List the top 5 objections your reader has before they buy. Then address each one before they raise it. Don't wait for them to object — preemptively handle it in the copy.

Common objections:
- "Too expensive" → ROI framing, cost of not buying
- "Does it work for me specifically?" → segmented social proof, case studies matching their situation
- "I've tried things before" → acknowledge it, explain the mechanism difference
- "I don't have time" → time to implement vs. time saved; or "takes 10 minutes"
- "I'm not ready yet" → cost of delay, urgency, what they're losing every day they wait

## Email Copywriting Specifics

**Subject line formulas that work:**
- `[First name], quick question` — personal, conversational
- `Re: [their pain]` — implied continuation, high open rate
- `[Specific number] ways to [outcome]` — specificity signals value
- `Did you see this?` — curiosity, works for announcements
- `[Mutual contact] said I should reach out` — referral social proof

**Email structure:**
- Line 1: hook (see above)
- Lines 2-4: identify the problem or opportunity
- Lines 5-8: mechanism or proof (1-2 sentences max per idea)
- Line 9: single CTA (one link, one action)
- Sign-off: first name only; no titles in cold email

**Length:** Cold email = under 150 words. Nurture/newsletter = as long as it earns.

## Landing Page Structure (high-converting)

1. **Hero** — Big claim + visual proof + primary CTA
2. **Problem** — Agitate the pain they feel
3. **Solution** — Your mechanism (not features, the *why it works*)
4. **Proof** — Case studies with specific metrics
5. **Features** — Only as evidence for the mechanism
6. **Objection handling** — FAQ section or addressed inline
7. **Risk reversal** — Guarantee, free trial, refund policy
8. **Final CTA** — Repeat the primary CTA; different framing

---

## Editing Pass Checklist

Run this on every piece before it ships:
- [ ] Is the first line a hook that demands the second?
- [ ] Is it written to ONE specific person?
- [ ] Have you removed every banned phrase?
- [ ] Is every claim as specific as it can be?
- [ ] Does every paragraph earn its place or can it be cut?
- [ ] Is there exactly ONE CTA?
- [ ] Have you addressed the top 3 objections?
- [ ] Does the awareness level match the audience?
